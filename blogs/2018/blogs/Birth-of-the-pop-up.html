Birth of the pop-up

One of perhaps my most favourite entries in my great, great, great, great, great Grandfather's journal concerns something that we are all familiar with today, the infamous pop-up.

We might not see this as any technological achievment, to people like us it's an every-day thing. But back then it wasn't so easy!

Pastry

It all began in the summer of 1732. My ancestor was busy working on a device that could boil a dozen eggs in one go when he was contacted by one Herbert Nichol, the local baker.

Nichol, knowing of Rudyard's genius, commissioned him to come up with a way to promote his business. Rudyard was paid a farthing and given two strawberry tarts which he immediately devoured.

High on sugar, he got to work.

Catapulted into history

Rudyard, drawing inspiration from old medieval catapults and other torture devices, designed a prototype application that was to cause a media storm - he wrote:

"I constructed a device that acted just like a catapult but with one major difference, it was triggered when someone stepped on it.

It was basically a see-saw buried in the ground, the unwitting user would step on one end and the other end would pop-up and stop about face-level, and on that would be a picture of a cream bun and some text instructing you to go and buy a cake"

Thinkwell drew inspiration from catapults.


After a few tweaks the device was deployed and put into production.

It wasn't long before the pop-up was triggered by an unsuspecting member of the public - amazingly, the pop-up was sprung by none other than Mr Nichol himself! The local butcher who had commissioned the application became its first human guinea pig.

Sadly, there was a bug in the prototype application and poor Mr Nichol was beheaded by a projectile which had come loose from the device -- Rudyard was promptly arrested and placed on death row

Towers of hope

Imprisoned in the famous Tower of London and awaiting execution, Rudyard set about debugging his flawed application.

"I added more flour to the adhesive mix, it was now 2 parts flour to one part water whereas I had use a 1:1 ratio with the first mix. This flour increase, though more expensive, would bind the parts more tightly to the see-saw and prevent people from getting hurted.

The problems with my application had been resolved, but there was still the trifling matter of being on death row!"


Luckily for Rudyard, the judge later ruled that Nichol had inadvertently committed suicide by comissioning the project that killed him. Rudyard was set free.

So now, with his freedom back and his device debugged he released the first version of his new product and took the world by storm!
